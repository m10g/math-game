📐 Meet the Triangles
Right-Angled Triangle
  • Has exactly one 90° angle (a right angle)
  • The right angle is marked with a small square corner
  • All three sides can be different lengths (3-4-5 is a famous example!)
💡
Look for the little square in the corner — that's the right angle mark! A right angle is like the corner of a book or a door frame.
Isosceles Triangle
  • Has 2 equal sides (marked with a single tick on each)
  • The 2 base angles (opposite the equal sides) are also equal
  • The third side (the base) is a different length
💡
Spot the tick marks on two sides — those sides are equal! "Iso" comes from Greek meaning equal.
Equilateral Triangle
  • All 3 sides are equal (each marked with a tick)
  • All 3 angles are equal — each is exactly 60°
  • It is perfectly symmetrical in 3 directions
  • ⭐ An equilateral triangle is also isosceles (it has at least 2 equal sides!)
💡
Key idea: Because an equilateral triangle has 3 equal sides, it automatically has at least 2 equal sides — so it qualifies as isosceles too! Every equilateral triangle is also isosceles.
Isosceles Right-Angled Triangle
  • Has one 90° right angle
  • The two legs (sides that meet at the right angle) are equal
  • The other two angles are each 45°
  • ⭐ It is both a right-angled triangle and an isosceles triangle

🔗 This triangle belongs to TWO families!

Isosceles right-angled → is also a Right-angled triangle
Isosceles right-angled → is also an Isosceles triangle
💡
Think of it as having two names at once — like someone who is both a sister and a student. It can correctly be called a right-angled triangle or an isosceles triangle!
Scalene Triangle
  • All 3 sides are different lengths — no tick marks
  • All 3 angles are different — no equal angles
  • It has no lines of symmetry
💡
No matching tick marks means no equal sides — it's a scalene! "Scalene" comes from Greek meaning unequal.
📏 The Triangle Rule

Not every set of 3 lengths can form a triangle! There is a rule you must check:

The Triangle Rule
The sum of any two sides must be greater than the third side.
You must check all three combinations!

✅ Example 1 — Sides: 3, 4, 5

3  •  4  •  5
3 + 4 = 7 > 5 ✓ Pass
3 + 5 = 8 > 4 ✓ Pass
4 + 5 = 9 > 3 ✓ Pass
All three pass! ✅ These CAN form a triangle.

❌ Example 2 — Sides: 1, 2, 10

1  •  2  •  10
1 + 2 = 3 > 10? ✗ FAIL (3 is not > 10)
1 + 10 = 11 > 2 ✓ Pass
2 + 10 = 12 > 1 ✓ Pass
One check failed! ❌ These CANNOT form a triangle.
💡
The shortest trick: just check if the two shorter sides added together beat the longest side. If they do, the other two checks always pass!

🗂️ How the Triangle Families Overlap

Equilateral ⊂ Isosceles (all equilateral triangles are also isosceles)
Iso. Right-Angled ⊂ Right-Angled (also right-angled)
Iso. Right-Angled ⊂ Isosceles (also isosceles)
Scalene stands on its own — no overlapping families
