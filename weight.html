⚖ Understanding Weight
Units of Weight

We measure how heavy things are using units of weight. The two most common units are:

Grams (g)
For light things
and
Kilograms (kg)
For heavy things

Examples of things measured in grams (g):

An apple
about 200 g
A basketball
about 600 g
A mobile phone
about 200 g

Examples of things measured in kilograms (kg):

A bag of rice
about 5 kg
A refrigerator
about 100 kg
A bicycle
about 15 kg
💡
Quick rule: If you can hold it easily with one hand, it's probably measured in grams. If it feels heavy or needs both hands, it's probably measured in kilograms.
Unit Conversion

Kilograms and grams are related. Knowing how to convert between them is an important skill!

The key rule to remember:
1 kg = 1000 g
One kilogram equals one thousand grams

Example 1: Simple conversion

Convert 2 kg to grams
2 kg = 2 × 1000 = 2000 g

Example 2: Mixed units

Convert 4 kg 50 g to grams
Step 1: 4 kg = 4 × 1000 = 4000 g
Step 2: 4000 g + 50 g = 4050 g

Example 3: Tricky mixed units

Convert 20 kg 20 g to grams
Step 1: 20 kg = 20 × 1000 = 20000 g
Step 2: 20000 g + 20 g = 20020 g
💡
Method: To convert X kg Y g to grams, first multiply the kg part by 1000, then add the grams part.
Formula: (X × 1000) + Y = total grams
Balance Scales

A balance scale compares the weight of objects. The heavier side goes down and the lighter side goes up.

  • If the left pan is lower, the left object is heavier
  • If the right pan is lower, the right object is heavier
  • If both pans are level, both objects weigh the same

Comparing three objects using two weighings:

C is heavier than A   (C > A)

A is heavier than B   (A > B)

Putting it together:
C > A > B    (Heaviest to lightest: C, A, B)

Using a balance scale with standard weights:

When the scale is balanced, the object on the left weighs the same as the total of the standard weights on the right.

Example: A pineapple balanced with 1 kg + 200 g + 200 g
Total = 1000 g + 200 g + 200 g = 1400 g
💡
Logic puzzles: If shapes are balanced against small circles, you can use the circles as a "common unit" to compare the shapes. For example, if a dome = 5 circles and a triangle = 8 circles, then the triangle is heavier than the dome!
Reading Dial Scales

Dial scales (also called weighing scales) have a needle that points to the weight. You need to read the markings carefully!

Step 1: Check the unit on the scale

  • Some scales show kg (kilograms)
  • Some scales show g (grams)
  • If the question asks for grams but the scale shows kg, you must convert!

Step 2: Work out what each small mark is worth

  • Find two numbers you can read on the scale
  • Count the small marks (intervals) between them
  • Divide the difference by the number of intervals
Example: Numbers 0 and 1 kg, with 10 intervals between them
Each small mark = 1 kg ÷ 10 = 0.1 kg = 100 g
Example: Numbers 0 and 200 g, with 4 intervals between them
Each small mark = 200 g ÷ 4 = 50 g

Step 3: Read the needle position

Example A: Needle at 4th mark after 0 (each mark = 100 g)
Weight = 4 × 100 g = 400 g
Example B: Needle at 1st mark before 2 kg (each mark = 0.2 kg)
Weight = 2 − 0.2 = 1.8 kg = 1800 g
Example C: Needle at the 800 mark on a gram scale
Weight = 800 g
💡
Always check three things:
1. What unit is the scale in? (kg or g)
2. What is each small mark worth?
3. Where is the needle pointing?
Comparing Weights

Sometimes you need to read two different scales and compare the weights. Make sure both weights are in the same unit first!

Example: Comparing a rice cooker and grapes

Rice cooker
4 kg = 4000 g
vs
Grapes
300 g
Step 1: Convert to the same unit
Rice cooker: 4 kg = 4000 g   |   Grapes: 300 g
Step 2: Find the difference
4000 g − 300 g = 3700 g
Step 3: Write the answer
The grapes are 3700 g lighter than the rice cooker.
💡
Remember: When comparing weights, always convert to the same unit first (usually grams). Then subtract the smaller from the larger to find the difference. The lighter object weighs less, the heavier object weighs more!
Measuring Tools

Different objects need different measuring tools. Choosing the right tool depends on what you're measuring and how heavy it is.

Kitchen Scale
Best for food, light items
Up to about 5 kg
Balance / Slider Scale
Best for small, precise items
Up to about 1 kg
Bathroom Scale
Best for people, very heavy items
Up to about 150 kg
Large Dial Scale
Best for medium items
Up to about 18 kg

How to choose the right tool:

  • Think about what you're measuring — weight needs a scale, not a ruler or syringe!
  • Estimate the object's weight — is it very light, medium, or heavy?
  • Match the range — the scale should be able to measure the object without being too big or too small

Quick guide:

  • A mobile phone (~200 g) → balance/slider scale (up to 1 kg) is best
  • A game console (~300 g) → kitchen scale is a good fit
  • A bag of rice (~5 kg) → kitchen or large dial scale
  • A person (~40 kg) → bathroom floor scale
💡
Elimination trick: First, cross out anything that doesn't measure weight (rulers, syringes, measuring tapes). Then pick the scale with the range that best fits the object!
